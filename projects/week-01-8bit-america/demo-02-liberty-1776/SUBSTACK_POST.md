# Summer Into AI Week 1 (Demo 2): The Declaration of Independence as a Space Invaders cabinet

After shipping Found a Republic, I had a thought: the Declaration of Independence is literally a list of grievances. It's already a wave structure.

So I built **Liberty 1776** — a Space Invaders-style arcade game where each wave is a real grievance from the Declaration. Round 1: Taxation Without Representation. Round 2: Quartering of Armed Troops. Round 3: Cutting Off Our Trade. Round 4: Dissolving Our Legislatures. Final boss: King George III himself, firing three-way volleys as his health bar depletes. Beat him and the screen explodes in 4th of July fireworks.

Pure canvas, pixel sprites hand-coded in character maps, 8-bit sound effects synthesized with the Web Audio API. No AI needed to play — but AI built every sprite, every wave brief, every line of the intel dispatches between rounds.

**Timestorm Baseball** by Eric Rhea ([@advisoryhour](https://advisoryhour.substack.com/p/summer-into-ai-timestorm-baseball)) showed me how well historical enemies work as game fodder — Founding Fathers vs. the British at Wrigley Field is genuinely inspired. Watching that, I realized the Declaration's grievance list was already a perfect level progression. They picked the matchup; I picked the format.

Also worth a look: **Parade Candy Chaos** by Kady Godfrey ([@kadygodfrey](https://kadygodfrey.substack.com)) — the first team entry in the competition, built with her 9-year-old. Proof that the best constraint isn't a deadline, it's a co-pilot who doesn't overthink it.
