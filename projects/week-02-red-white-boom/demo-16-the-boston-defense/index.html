Boston, Massachusetts Bay Colony — 1770

On the night of March 5th, 1770, British soldiers under Captain Thomas Preston fired into a crowd on King Street. Five colonists lay dead in the snow. Boston cries murder. The trial of the century has begun.

The prosecution will call five witnesses. You will cross-examine each one. Then twelve men will decide the fate of the accused.

The Verdict of the Jury
⚖ What Actually Happened

On December 5, 1770, the jury returned its verdict after less than three hours of deliberation. Six soldiers were acquitted of all charges. Two soldiers — Hugh Montgomery and Matthew Kilroy — were found guilty of manslaughter (not murder). They were branded on the thumb and released.

John Adams won the most important case of his legal career. He argued before a city demanding blood and convinced a colonial jury to follow the law instead of their grief. He later called it “one of the best pieces of service I ever rendered my country.”

The trial established a principle that would define the new nation: no person — not even an enemy soldier — was above the law, and none was beneath its protection. This was the first independence engine: not a musket, but a gavel.
